Impacts of Automation

AI based computer systems are widely used and already make a lot of decisions for us in sectors such as banking, HR and legal services. In business, AI systems help to carry out financial transactions and aid HR departments in screening job applicants. We also rely on Al based systems for individualised online purchasing recommendations, keep track of our physical health with wearable technology, and live in homes with "smart" technologies that control the lighting, temperature, entertainment systems, and appliances.

Unfortunately, as these systems significantly impact many businesses and organizations, they also create many challenges. Algorithm biases, discrimination or privacy invasions, unexplainable outcomes, and/or lack of accountability have been identified in many AI based systems.  For example, predictive models in justice (Larson et al. 2016), search engines (Kay et al. 2015), and predictive models in healthcare (Obermeyer et al. 2019). In Netherlands, around 40,000 families unfairly suffered profound financial harm and other damages due to the tax authorities’ reliance on an awed AI system to identify potential fraudulent use of a child benefit tax-relief program. The issue is that these systems were limited by the specific goals for which they were created, disregarding potentially harmful consequences that violate our moral standards of justice and fairness.

Consequently, AI ethics have grown to be a significant concern for society, and the ethical use of AI systems has been adopted by most nations, universities, policymakers, businesses, and non-profit organisations. According to Moser et. al (2022), we should be very wary about replacing human judgement:
Those who believe in ethical AI consider the technology to be a tool, on par with other tools that are essentially extensions of the human body, such as lenses (to extend the view of the eyes), or spoons and screwdrivers (to extend the dexterity of the hands). But we are sceptical. In our view, as with wearables, it's crucial to consider the user as part of the tool: We need to think hard about how algorithms shape us.

What we believe is "right" risks becoming no longer a question of ethics but simply what the "correct" result of a mathematical calculation is.

Moser et. al. 2022
