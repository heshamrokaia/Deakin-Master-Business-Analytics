Underlying General Principles for Algorithmic Transparency and Accountability

Accountability, and transparency of AI systems requires thinking beyond the technical ‘decision’, and including the broader decision system of technology, people, and institutions. The Association for Computing Machinery (ACM)’s United States and European Public Policy Councils have described seven principles of Accountability and Transparency. Those seven principals are awareness; access and redress; accountability; explanation; data provenance; auditability; and validation and testing.

Awareness

Awareness is educating the public and other parties involved with analytical systems (owners, designers, developers, users) should be aware of any potential biases present during these stages of development as well as the potential harm that these biases may do to people and society as a whole.

Access and Redress

Access and Redress means that the existence of a process for looking into and revising incorrect judgments or decisions 

Accountability

Accountability means institutions should be held responsible for decisions made by the algorithms that they use, even if it is not feasible to explain in detail how the algorithms produce their results. Accountability rejects the frequently used redirection of blame to an automated system by ensuring those who deploy an algorithm cannot avoid responsibility for its actions 

Explanation

Explanation means the logic of the algorithm must be communicable in human terms no matter how complex it is (Garfinkel et al., 2017). This encourages, the production of explanations for both the algorithm's actions and the specific judgments made for systems and institutions that use algorithmic decision making.

Data Provenance

Data Provenance ensures the reliability and trustworthiness of data. The selection of training datasets will affect judgments because many modern methodologies rely on statistical analyses of large pool of collected data. It is also crucial to understand the sources of the data and the reliability or provenance of the data.

Auditability

Auditability refers to the ability to audit models, algorithms, data, and decisions when harm is suspected. It is necessary to maintain logs and records for the purpose of auditing a decision-making system, such as for resolving disputes or ensuring compliance with regulatory requirements 

Validation and Testing 

Validation and Testing of automated systems should be conducted on an ongoing basis using techniques such as regression tests, corner case analysis, and red-teaming strategies that are used in computer security to increase the confidence in automated systems.

Adapted from: Garfinkel et al. (2017). Toward Algorithmic Transparency and Accountability. Communications of the ACM 60(9): 5. doi.org/10.1145/3125780.
Matthews, J. (2020). Patterns and Anti-Patterns, Principles and Pitfalls: Accountability and Transparency in AI. AI Magazine, 41(1), 82-89.

The seven principles of Accountability and Transparency from ACM are built on the three key principles of Accountability, Responsibility and Transparency (ART). You’ll be exploring them in greater detail next. 

Even though each of the ART principles can apply to all aspects of AI systems, each is imperative for a specific characteristic (Dignum, 2019). Therefore, a truly responsible artificial intelligence cannot be autonomous without some form of responsibility, cannot be interactive without accountability, nor can it be adaptive without transparency. In order to be successful, ART requires training and awareness of all stakeholders, including researchers, designers, programmers, managers, providers, users, and all other stakeholders.
