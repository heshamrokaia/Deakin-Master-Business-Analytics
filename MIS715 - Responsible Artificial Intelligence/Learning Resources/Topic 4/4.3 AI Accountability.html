AI Accountability

According to OECD (2022), accountability refers to:

The expectation that organisations and individuals who design, develop, operate or deploy AI systems collect relevant data and ensure that AI systems function properly throughout their lifecycle. They are also expected to do all this in accordance with their roles and applicable regulatory frameworks and should also demonstrating this through their actions and decision-making process.

AI accountability also implies the ability of a system to explain and justify its actions to its users and other stakeholders (Dignum,2019). Consequently, the mechanisms used for making decisions must be derivable from, and explainable by, the decision-making process. The emphasis on accountability must go beyond technical artefacts and encompass how those technical artefacts relate to broader decision-making processes, such as the development and implementation processes (Goldenfein, 2019). Accountability covers ethical, moral, or other expectations (e.g., as set out in management practices or codes of conducts) that guide the actions/conduct of individuals or organisations in designing and using AI systems.

Do you think this lack of accountability in computerised systems (of which AI is one) has come about suddenly? Or is it something that has slowly become the norm?

Helen Nissenbaum (1996) warned two decades ago about the erosion of accountability in our computerised society and of four trends that obscure accountability. The first problem is that computerised systems are typically designed and operated by several parties. This leads to the dissemination of responsibility over a wide range of parties, and the ability to analyse who or what institutions can be held accountable is obscured. As a second consideration, the inevitability of bugs in software, as problematic as it is, is also used as an excuse to stop focusing on faulty software as a general issue. Consequently, it becomes difficult to conduct a comprehensive analysis of software bugs and hinders the investment of resources to eliminate and prevent them. As a third point, we are prone to blaming the computer rather than the humans involved when things happen wrong. Lastly, the lack of accountability on the part of software developers is not helpful.

We are living in an era where algorithms are used to determine most of our life decisions such as loan approvals and insurance premiums. They are also used to detect tax evasion, money laundering, drug trafficking, smuggling, and terrorist activity. They influence the information we receive, the advertisements we are shown, the recommendations we are recommended, etc. A great deal of our lives is influenced by such algorithms. However, their outcomes suffer from some notable defects. Individual outcomes can be unfair or vary arbitrarily between algorithms. Garfinkel and others (2017) mentioned that the outcomes of an algorithms may be biased toward specific groups (on the basis of gender, race, and similar characteristics). The most troubling aspect of this algorithmic decision-making is that almost all aspects remain opaque. Therefore, De Laat (2018) noted that in addition to the four barriers already identified,  algorithmic decision-making is the fifth barrier to proper accountability related to computerised systems.

All participants in the process should be involved in eliciting the moral values and societal norms that inform the purpose of the system, as well as their operational interpretations. The organisations creating and using algorithms can be held accountable through governance systems, despite the fact that algorithms themselves cannot be held responsible because they are neither moral nor legal actors (Raji, et all, 2020).
