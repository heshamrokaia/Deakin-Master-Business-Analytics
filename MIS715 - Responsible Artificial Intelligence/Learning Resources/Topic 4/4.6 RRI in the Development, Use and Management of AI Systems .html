RRI in the Development, Use and Management of AI Systems 

Responsible Research and Innovation (RRI) is defined as a:

transparent, interactive process by which societal actors and innovators become mutually responsive to each other with a view to the (ethical) acceptability, sustainability and societal desirability of the innovation process and its marketable product

Dignum, 2019, p. 50

As the name implies, RRI is a process for conducting research and innovation that takes into account the effects on the environment and society as well as the potential for impact. The development of AI systems can be guided by an RRI vision. 

 Note In topic 2.4 we introduced RRI and how AI research could use it to reduce its impacts on the environment and society. Explore topic 2.4 Responsible AI Research and Discussion.

What are the Key Elements of RRI?

Seven key elements constitute RRI, and these 'key elements' are necessarily associated with ethics and governance (Wilford et al, 2016):

  • Doing the Right Thing (Ethics)
  • Good and Reflexive Governance
  • Creative Learning (Science Education)
  • Choosing Together (Engagement and Involvement)
  • Unlocking the Full Potential (Gender Equality)
  • Sharing Results (Open Access)
  • Taking Care of our Planet (Environmental) Stewardship

As mentioned by Dignum (2019), AI can only be understood and fully benefited by everyone once its impact has been clearly demonstrated. As a result, RRI in AI should include steps to ensure a wide and thorough education for all stakeholders, both present and future, as well as governance models for AI's responsibilities.

Dignum (2019) highlights several reasons why its important that we take an RRI approach when developing AI systems. The autonomous nature of AI systems potentially supports the evolution of these systems into ones with undesirable effects. Despite their performances, AI systems are not yet considered as reliable enough to be fully autonomous in complex environments without human supervision as some AI algorithms can make or affect decisions with direct and important consequences on people’s lives. 

It is also important to consider the ethical implications of machine decision-making, and to establish the legal status of AI. RRI in AI should ensure widespread societal support for the AI applications that are being developed by focusing on human values and well-being. 

RRI in AI use and management

According to Candelon et al (2021), organizations relying on human decision-makers will still need to control for unconscious bias among those people, which AI can help reveal. To address biased outcomes, it is important to not rely only on standard antidiscrimination legislation as it is only workable when there are people who can be held responsible for problematic decisions. But with AI increasingly in the mix, individual accountability is undermined. 

In the worse scenario, when AI increases the potential scale of bias, any flaw could potentially affect millions of people, exposing companies to class-action lawsuits of historic proportions and putting their reputations at risk, such as the victims of Robodebt  are still suffering from the outcome of the system’s failure!

What can executives do to head off such problems?

Prior to making any decision, they should deepen their understanding of AI systems, by exploring the impacts of outcomes, the nature and scope of decisions, operational complexity and limits to scale and the level of explanation required. They can consider developing and applying AI policies with commitments to safety, fairness, diversity, and privacy.
