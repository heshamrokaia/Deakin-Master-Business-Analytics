Enlightenment - Consequentialist theory

Consequentialist theory (i.e., utilitarianism) argues that consequences of one’s action are the ultimate basis for judgement of a right (versus wrong) action. An action is right (or wrong) to the extent that it increases (or decreases) the total happiness of the affected parties. Therefore, an action is good if it benefits, bad if it harms. Consequences of the action are paramount, not the motive. Rule utilitarianism applies the principles of utility to universal moral rules and act utilitarianism applies the principles of utility to individual moral actions. Appropriate rule for universal application is ‘if I can write and release a helpful worm that improves the security of the computers it infects, I should do it”. If everyone does this, there will be extra network traffic, making computers unusable, good worms may contain bugs, and extra work on administrators. So, action of the person is morally wrong. Act utilitarianism is only concerned with the act, and pros, and cons, of releasing the specific worm only. If it does improve security, it might be seen as moral. Arguments against Consequentialist theory is that it forces a single measure to evaluate all different kinds of consequences ignoring the problem of unjust distribution of good consequences.
