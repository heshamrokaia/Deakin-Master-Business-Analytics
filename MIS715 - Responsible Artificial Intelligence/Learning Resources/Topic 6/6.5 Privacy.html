Privacy

Privacy, in the context of AI ethics, refers to the extent to which people have knowledge and control over their data without undue pressure. Privacy issues have received a fair amount of media coverage in recent years, with citizens, consumers and employees demanding how governments should regulate issues regarding privacy. There have been some countries (such as Australia, UK, France) that have implemented regulations. Regulatory compliance, integrity and security data, and ethics are the major aspects to privacy (Blackman, 2022). 

Four elements of ethical treatment of privacy 

floatleft

Transparency

 Users, customers, or citizens should be able to know or to control what information is being collected about them, what’s being done with it, what decisions they contribute to, who it’s being shared with or sold to. If organisations didn’t advise customers about those, or worse, they don’t know either, that’s an ethical problem.

floatleft

Data control

Giving the capacity for users, customers, or citizen to be able to correct, edit, or delete information about themselves and to opt out of being treated in a certain way (e.g., opting out of targeted ads) is part of what is involved in exercising control over one’s information. By doing so, the organisation shows their respect to the stakeholders’ privacy and provides the stakeholders an opportunity to fix incorrect information the organisation has about them. As a result, the organisation will be able to create a better AI model and serve them better.

floatleft

Opt out by default

Without users’ consent, many organisations currently collect data by default via the registration process and as a user remains active on the company’s site (e.g., from where users go on to the site, how long they stay there for, what they click on, etc.). This means that users are automatically "opted-in” for the data collection about them by the organisations raising another ethical issue of privacy.

floatleft

Full services offered

Organisations may also increase or decrease the services they provide based on what data a user provides. For example, some organisations will not provide any services if users don’t consent to their privacy policies. Some will vary the degree of services provided depending on how much data the user shares. Some others will provide full services independently of how much data the user shares. The ethical issue of privacy will occur depending on how essential the services are. If the services are essential and an organisation only provides the services with the condition that you provide them with an access to your data that you are not comfortable with, then this raises an ethical issue of privacy.

Based on these four aspects of privacy (i.e., Transparency, Data control, Opt-out by default, and Full services offered), Blackman (2022 p. 99) categorised them into five ethical levels of privacy including Blindfolded and handcuffed, Handcuffed, Pressured, Slightly curtailed, and Grateful.

Note that, there would be cases where organisations are committed to different levels for different products and services and they might be transparent in this respect but not in another respect (e.g., transparent about what data is collected but opaque about what predictions they are making from the collected data).

How organisations should get it right: 

  • As a business, before you start collecting data to train your AI model, determine what ethical level of privacy is appropriate for the use case.
  • Your business needs an individual or ideally, a set of individuals, that can make responsible, expert-informed decisions when ethical values conflict.
