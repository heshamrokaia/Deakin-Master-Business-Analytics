AI Ethical Frameworks

In this section, we will discuss several AI Ethical frameworks that are relevant to our context nationally and internationally.

AI regulation with EU AI Act

One of very first attempt to regulate AI is the AI Act developed and proposed by the European Union (EU) to provide developers, deployers, and users with clear requirements and obligations regarding specific uses of AI. The EU Commission proposed the AI Act as the first-ever legal framework on AI addressing the risk of AI and positions EU countries to play a leading role globally. The Act has been approved by the Council of the European Union. 

The text below was extracted from EU AI Act, and the Act can be accessed on the AI Act page

The law assigns applications of AI to three risk categories. First, applications and systems that create an unacceptable risk, such as government-run social scoring of the type used in China, are banned. Second, high-risk applications, such as a CV-scanning tool that ranks job applicants, are subject to specific legal requirements. Lastly, applications not explicitly banned or listed as high-risk are largely left unregulated.

UNESCO’s Recommendation on AI Ethics

Being aware of the urgent need for international and national policies and regulatory frameworks to ensure that these emerging technologies benefit humanity as a whole, in November 2021, at UNESCO’s General Conference, UNESCO Member states approved the adoption of the first ever global agreement on the Ethics of AI called The Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence. This is the very first global standard-setting instrument on the subject to not only protect but also promote human rights and human dignity, but it also serves as an ethical guiding compass and a global normative bedrock allowing to build strong respect for the rule of law in the digital world.

The Recommendation aims to provide a basis to make AI systems work for the good of humanity, individuals, societies and the environment and ecosystems, and to prevent harm. It also aims at stimulating the peaceful use of AI systems.

UNESCO’s Recommendations focus on the aspects of Proportionality and Do No Harm; Safety and security, Fairness and non-discrimination, Sustainability, Right to Privacy, and Data Protection, Human oversight and determination, transparency and explainability, Responsibility and accountability, Awareness and literacy, Multi-stakeholder and adaptive governance and collaboration.

Australia’s AI Ethics Framework

Department of Industry, Science, and Resources in Australia has developed an AI Ethics Framework to guide businesses and governments to responsibly design, develop and implement AI. The framework aims to make Australia a global leader in responsible and inclusive AI and to obtain the immense potential of AI by making sure it is safe, secure and reliable to use.
