‘The right to life’ is considered a fundamental right of humans in the constitutions of 77% of the world’s countries as recognised by the United Nations (UN 2018).  Countries who are signatories to human rights treaties are obligated to take appropriate measures to safeguard individuals from any violations of their right to life, i.e. individuals are not subjected to any form of harm that may result in the loss or removal of their life without legal process.

While AI has the potential to support an individual’s enjoyment of life, liberty and security, there are also potential threats that must be addressed. Some of these AI threats include threats to privacy, introduce biases and manipulation of individuals. The European Commission (2021) has identified AI systems in several areas as high-risk including:

  • critical infrastructure such as transportation,
  • educational or vocational training which may impact access to education and career advancement,
  • safety components of products such as AI application in robot-assisted surgery, 
  • employment and worker management,
  • access to essential private and public services,
  • law enforcement that may impact fundamental rights, 
  • migration, asylum and border control management and
  • the administration of justice and democratic processes.

The potential impact may vary but nonetheless relevant. The use of robot-assisted medical procedures and robotics systems in surgery (Alemzadeh et al. 2016), robot accidents and malfunctions in manufacturing, law enforcement (Boyd 2016), retail and entertainment settings (Jiang and Gainer 1987), security vulnerabilities in smart home hubs (Fránik and ˇCermák 2020), self-driving and autonomous vehicles (AP and Reuters 2021), and lethal attacks by AI-armed drone swarms and autonomous weapons (Safi 2019) have all raised life-threatening concerns.
