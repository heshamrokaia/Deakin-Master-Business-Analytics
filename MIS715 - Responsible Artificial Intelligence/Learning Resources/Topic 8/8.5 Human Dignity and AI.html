Human Dignity and AI

In this section, we will look closely into a specific and significant aspect of human rights, i.e., human dignity. Human dignity refers to the inherent worth and value of every human being, encompassing various aspects such as autonomy, respect and privacy, regardless of their individual characteristics, such as age, race, gender, or abilities.

Since AI becomes more advanced and pervasive in our lives, it has the potential to impact these aspects of human dignity.  In other words, human dignity is a critical consideration in the era of AI as the technology can potentially impact many aspects of human life, from employment to healthcare to security. If AI systems are designed or used in a way that undermines human dignity, it can have serious consequences for individuals and society as a whole.

What is human dignity?

Each human being possesses inherent human dignity, which is considered the foundation of humanity and requires social protection (Pogge, 2014). The concept of dignity can be understood in three ways in philosophy: dignity as character or bearing, dignity as status, and dignity as intrinsic value (Rosen, 2012).  Behavioural dignity is the term used to describe the first aspect of dignity, referring to the idea that an individual’s dignity is reflected in the way they live their life, particularly in their actions, attitudes and behaviours. This notion of dignity emphasises the importance of living a virtuous life, which involves cultivating positive character traits and virtues such as honesty, compassion, courage and respect of others (Ober, 2014).

According to Greek philosophers, behavioural dignity is characterised by four cardinal virtues of wisdom, courage, moderation, and justice as outlined in Plato’s Republic and further developed by Aristotle. In the views of Thomas Aquinas and the early Christian theologians, behavioural dignity is defined as individuals accepting their appropriate position in a divine order without opposition. The concept of behavioural dignity is a social construct because society, family, and/or friends, and especially those who hold power to influence others through persuasive arguments, incentives or coercion have historically determined what constitutes virtuous behaviour.

Human rights documents commonly safeguard the inherent dignity of human beings. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights, for instance, declares that 

recognition of the inherent dignity and of the equal and inalienable rights of all members of the human family is the foundation of freedom, justice and peace in the world

United Nations, 1948

Similarly, the first article of the German Constitution (Grundgesetz) affirms that

(1) Human dignity shall be inviolable. To respect and protect it shall be the duty of all state authority

Tomuschat et al., 1949; Germany 1949

Given the focus of this section on artificial intelligence (AI) ethics cases that relate to potential human rights infringements, one might think that the concept of dignity would be very useful. And indeed, as will be seen below, dignity has made an entrance into AI ethics discussions.

However, the usefulness of dignity in ethical debates is often challenged by some critics. The question of how useful dignity can be in AI ethics discussions is the focus of this topic.

In the context of AI ethics, dignity can be a conversation stopper if it is used as an absolute value that takes precedence over all other ethical considerations. On the other hand, it can be helpful in explaining and resolving ethical dilemmas related to AI if it is seen as an equal among equals, that is, as one of the many ethical values that need to be considered in evaluating the potential benefits and harms of AI technologies. The two cases discussed in the following section illustrate the potential relevance of dignity in AI ethics debates. The first case involves the groundless dismissal of an employee by an automated system, while the second case relates to care robots and the importance of ensuring they respect the dignity of the elderly and other vulnerable groups. The conclusion drawn from these cases is that dignity is a crucial value in human rights frameworks, especially in extreme cases such as torture, human trafficking, slavery and reproductive manipulation. However, in AI ethics debates, it should be viewed as one of the many ethical values that need to be taken into account. (Stahl et al., 2023)
