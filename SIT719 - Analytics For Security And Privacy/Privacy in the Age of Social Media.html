Privacy in the age of social media

We go to social media sites to connect with our friends. But these sites are also in the business of connecting us to advertisers - sometimes whether we consent or not.

In 2010, a Wall Street Journal investigation revealed that apps on the social-networking site Facebook had been transmitting member’s names and, sometimes their friends’ names, to web advertising and tracking companies.

The investigation highlighted:

  • the need for users to be vigilant about the privacy of their information on social media
  • the complexities created as a result of the involvement of third parties that may compromise the agreements that social media networks may have with their members
  • the challenges faced by the regulatory bodies when dealing with fully internet-based entities such as Facebook that operate outside of country-based jurisdictions
  • the importance of the media in highlighting privacy breaches or the possibility of privacy breaches in a timely manner.

Imagine you are a security consultant hired by a leading business to oversee the implementation of social media-based strategies, including the use of apps to generate a greater connection with their existing and potential customers.

You can use the issues raised by the Facebook investigation to advise your customer about the implications of using web apps. You can think about the policies and practices to put in place to avoid potential risks that include reputational damage, regulatory penalties and loss of trust.
