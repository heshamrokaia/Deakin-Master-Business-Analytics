The impact of new technologies on privacy

As willing users of new technologies are we creating difficulties for the preservation of existing forms of privacy?

In the article Privacy is Dead, Long Live Privacy, Jean-Pierre Hubaux and Ari Juels argue that we need to prepare for a ‘post-confidentiality world’.

We should prepare for the possibility of a post-confidentiality world, one in which confidentiality has greatly eroded and in which data flows in such complicated ways that social norms are jeopardized…There remains a vital and enduring place for confidentiality. Particularly in certain niches—protecting political dissent, anti-censorship in repressive regimes—it can play a societally transformative role. (p. 41).

Hubaux and Juels highlight that this radical shift in the privacy landscape comes from a number of different sources:

  1. Key notions of privacy enshrined in laws, policies, and ordinary conduct in almost every culture and social setting.
  2. Capabilities of privacy-enhancing technologies.
  3. New technologies that challenges the preservation of existing norms of privacy.
  4. Major trends providing the means, motive, and opportunity for the ‘assault’ on privacy.

Typically, a security consultant puts forward a number of reasons why mapping is necessary for the determination of the most appropriate security technique. Please read the article by Read Hubaux and Juels with the following issues in mind.

  1. Identify and describe the capabilities of privacy-enhancing technologies.
  2. Identify and describe the new technologies that challenges the preservation of existing norms of privacy.
  3. Identify and explain major trends providing the means, motive and opportunity for the ‘assault’ on privacy.
