What is privacy?

Historically, privacy is considered a difficult term to define as it is understood differently by different people in different contexts.

For those who intend to work in the ‘information’ industry involved in the creation, distribution, and consumption of information, there is a growing necessity to understand privacy, avoid its breaches and plan for its protection.

As organisations analyse customer behaviour to gather greater insights and stay ahead of the competition, there are major challenges that they need to overcome, especially in regards to complying with government regulation.

The issues covered that we will look at:

  1. The definition of privacy. While privacy is mentioned quite often alongside analytics and other areas of interest, there is no universally accepted definition. We will explain the causes and define privacy accordingly.
  2. Recognising the significance of privacy, governments have enacted laws that govern privacy and related issues. In this regard, we will look at ways to comply with such regulatory mechanisms.

 Privacy and analytics

Privacy is an important aspect of people’s lives. But what exactly is it?

As the information industry grows through technologies such as social media, Internet of Things, and cloud computing, the topic of privacy is widely getting sustained attention.

The streams of data being generated through these technologies and their use in the analytics have created increased public discussion about privacy. However, there is no universally accepted definition for privacy. It is defined differently depending on the context in which it is being used.

One of the first seminal works on privacy was Parker’s (1974) paper, A definition of privacy. To summarise Parker’s view on how to define privacy, he states that any definition should be judged based on the following criteria

  • How neatly it encompasses commonly agreed losses of privacy, without being too broad or too narrow.
  • The simplicity of the definition
  • How easy it is to make use of in the justice system

Parker’s definition says “privacy is control over when and by whom the various parts of us can be sensed by others.”
